The Dawn of AI: Joseph Weizenbaum's ELIZA

Created by computer scientist Joseph Weizenbaum at MIT in 1966, ELIZA was one of the first programs to convincingly simulate human conversation. Its most famous script, DOCTOR, mimicked a Rogerian psychotherapist by rephrasing a user's statements into questions.

The ELIZA Effect: More Than a Program

Weizenbaum was shocked to find that users, including his own secretary, began to attribute human-like intelligence, feelings, and empathy to the program, even while knowing it was a simple script. This phenomenon became known as the "ELIZA Effect."

The Moral Compass: Weizenbaum's Concerns

Weizenbaum became a vocal critic of artificial intelligence. He was deeply disturbed by the "powerful delusional thinking" ELIZA could induce and warned against the dehumanization of human interaction. He argued that computers should never be used in roles that require genuine human understanding and empathy.

Legacy: A Foundation for Modern AI Ethics

ELIZA's simple design laid the foundation for modern chatbots and natural language processing. More importantly, Weizenbaum's ethical concerns sparked a critical debate that continues to this day, influencing discussions about AI safety, accountability, and the responsible use of technology in society.
